# Software Engineering: Technical Interview Alignment to the Career Development Framework

## Career Development Framework Alignment

The interview questions we ask are designed to measure a candidate's technical proficiency with the skills required to be successful within Build for the role for which they are interviewing. They are also a way to measure a candidate's technical level within our roles as we have defined them in our Career Development Framework. [(CDF)](https://twodegrees1.sharepoint.com/teams/Build/SitePages/Career-Framework.aspx)

The levels in CDF are defined as a 1\-5 scale as follows:



| **Level** | **Description** | **Definition** | **Example** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Beginner | Someone operating at a beginner level in various skills or capabilities will generally be fairly ‘shallow and slim’: They likely know trivial answers to conceptual questions assuming they are familiar with the concept at all. Additionally, they will not have started to form the associations with related concepts. They can influence an assigned scope of work. | A software engineer is a **beginner** in **quality** when they can write and maintain unit tests. |
| 2 | Foundational | Someone at the foundational level with a skill will be able to help others perform that skill. They are starting to develop some depth in particular skills, but that depth will not be met by other skills. They can articulate different flavors of a concept. They can influence their assigned scope of work while also lending a hand to other team mates. | A software engineer has developed a **foundational** level in **quality** when they are able to define their own test scenarios and they are able to help their team mates do the same. |
| 3 | Intermediate | Someone at the intermediate level with a skill has a good understanding of that particular concept and they are starting to draw correlations between that concept and other concepts. They are able to guide a team through an aggregate scope of work | A software engineer is developing an **intermediate** level of understanding of **quality** when they are able to see how different types of testing make up a holistic picture of the verifiability of a component. |
| 4 | Advanced | Someone at an advanced level with a skill is developing a depth and breadth of that concept. They can articulate different approaches to reach the same goal and they understand the overlapping influences and impacts of related concepts. They are able to direct multiple work streams through an aggregated scope of work toward consistent application of patterns and practices | A software engineer is developing **advanced** skills in **quality** when they are able to associate the benefit of design patterns such as Dependency Injection or Inversion of Control to the activity of writing tests. |
| 5 | Expert | When operating at an advanced level with a skill, the team member has breadth and depth across many different concepts. They are able to draw from that breadth to understand the impacts of decitions between various concepts. They are able to influence the strategy and direction across multiple projects. | A software engineer has **expert** skills in **quality** when they can plan, articulate to the team and implement a multi\-faceted quality approach that takes advantage of different types of testing, holistic code quality and automation in order to realize the validation goals of their project. They are helping to define best practices and approaches that can be applied to other projects. |
