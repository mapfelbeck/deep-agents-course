## Problem Solving Conversation Flow

This section covers the standard problem solving flow for the Software Engineering Technical Interview. 

The Technical Interview is broken into three sections, and will vary depending on the level of the candidate is interviewing for. 

### **Introduction**

Our objective during this introduction is to build rapport, put the candidate at ease, and set clear expectations on the flow of the interview (ie. “*We have an aggressive schedule, and we’ll need to move through the sections quickly so don’t be discouraged if i cut you off…*”). For more context, see Technical Interview.

### **Problem Solving Question(s)**

The structure of the interview will change at this section depending on the level of the candidate. 

#### **Velocity Roles (Engineer / Senior Engineer)**

For Engineers and Senior Engineers, we are assessing the candidate on their ability to perform effectively on a team. Our primary focus here is to determine whether they can effectively contribute to velocity on the projects we work on; in parallel, we should also be evaluating whether this is someone that can work at a Slalom client project and with a Slalom Build team. A typical flow looks like this:

* **Coding Problem (20\-30 mins).** Determine the candidate’s ability to solve a coding problem. We do *not* want the candidate to use coding assistants (GitHub Copilot, Q Developer, etc) so that we can ascertain their depth of knowledge and skills, not just their ability to use tools. It is highly encouraged that the candidate performing using a program language that we typically use (i.e., Java, C\#, JavaScript) \- avoid using pseudo\-code. Through this exercise, we are looking to get a sense of:


	+ How quickly, correctly and effectively can the candidate write code?
	+ Can the candidate think creatively, out of the box, and adapt to dynamic situations?
	+ Can the candidate explain their thought process clearly, and justify decisions they are making?
	+ Is the candidate asking clarifying questions, understanding constraints?
* **Entity / Data Model Problem (15\-20 mins).** Determine the candidate’s ability to map the problem scenario into a relational entity / data model. In this exercise, we are assessing the following:


	+ What is the candidate’s ability to organize their solution around a conceptual entity / data model? Does the suggested model support the solution requirements? How resilient is the model to future changes and enhancements?
	+ Does the candidate understand modelling concepts, i.e.. entities, types, relationships?
	+ How does the conceptual model translate to the physical model?
	+ What is the candidate’s ability to write SQL (i.e.. INNER/OUTER joins, aggregates, group by, etc..)
* **Modern Web (optional).** Determine the candidate’s ability to build applications using modern web technologies (Single Page Applications \- Angular, React, Vue). 


	+ What is the candidate’s level of understanding around Single Page Application Concepts?
	+ Does the candidate know how to structure a front end application with extensibility and resiliency in mind?
	+ Does the candidate understand how to integrate front end applications with back end services (i.e.. REST, Graph)?

#### **Architect Roles (Architect / Senior Architect / Principal / Director)**

For Architect\+ roles, we are assessing the candidate on their ability to lead a development team and drive architecture design on projects. We are expecting these candidates to be able to go both deep (strong technical expertise in software development concepts) and wide (broad understanding of technologies across the architecture disciplines). Their ability to lead, and confidence in communicating architectural concepts and decisions need to be reflected throughout the interview process. The conversation flow looks as follows:

* **Coding Problem (15\-25 mins).** See *Coding Problem under Velocity Roles for context.* We expect candidates at the architect\+ levels to be able to solve the coding problem with ease. We are gauging the candidate on ability to think outside the box. We do *not* want the candidate to use coding assistants (GitHub Copilot, Q Developer, etc) so that we can ascertain their depth of knowledge and skills, not just their ability to use tools.. These are folks that would typically be performing code reviews, driving code quality through enforcement of unit testing and code analysis. Do they have a good understanding of these concepts?
* **Architectural Problem (20\-30 mins).** Determine the candidate’s capability to lead and drive the design of a technical solution. *What is our confidence level putting the candidate in an Architect capacity on one of our programs?* 


	+ What is the candidate’s level of expertise across both software and technical architecture concepts? (ie. application arch, system arch, cloud arch, devops, quality, security, scale, data, integration, governance, etc..)
	+ How deep can the candidate go into each of these concepts? (ie. frameworks, libraries, technical approaches, experiences)
	+ What’s the driver behind the candidate’s ability to make technology decisions? Does the candidate have a good foundation on the *Why* behind some of these technologies?
	+ How does the candidate find balance across technical and non\-technical constraints in landing on a solution?
	+ How does the candidate translate architecture into delivery? What has been their experience?
	+ How is the candidate’s confidence level and communication skills? Can we see them driving a technology conversation with our team and and our clients?
* **GenAI (optional, as part of the architectural problem).** Determine the candidate’s depth of knowledge in architecting and designing GenAI.


	+ What is the candidate’s level of expertise in architecting applications that include or rely on GenAI?
	+ What concepts are they familiar with? (Prompt engineering, context, RAG, fine tuning, embeddings, chains/agents/skills/tools, MLOps, testing)
	+ How well does the candidate understand the purpose and functionality of GenAI tools and frameworks, not just the application of those tools.

### **Candidate Questions and Next Steps**

* Provide the candidate an opportunity to ask questions. For more context, see Technical Interview.
* Wrap up conversation and conduct debrief or close interview if you are the last with instructions on next steps.
