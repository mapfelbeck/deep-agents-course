# Why Problem Solving?

The purpose of the initial interview is to understand a candidate's ability to be successful in Build and level them for the correct role: it is the first step toward assessing their **technical proficiency**. As you conduct an initial interview, make sure to ask ' why' questions that elicit a conversation so you can have a more in\-depth understanding of their thought process. If you only ask 'what' questions, e.g., what are generics or what are promises, it is difficult to have a concrete understanding of whether a candidate can apply these concepts in their work. Therefore, each section of the initial interview should include 1 \- 2 places to "dig in" and ask the candidate why or how they would apply the concept in their day\-to\-day work.

Delivering the interview question in ways that tap into their core understanding of a concept only gets us partially there \- we are also seeking clues as to their **curiosity** and **consulting presence**. To that end, we also need to look at candidates' non\-technical cues as a validation of level. Their ability to communicate concepts effectively could indicate their effectiveness in persuading a client or a team member in a project scenario, which is an essential aspect of our day\-to\-day work. A "why" question gives them the stage to demonstrate their ability here. It's up to us to translate their performance into an assessment of whether they can perform effectively at the expected level. 

Additionally, asking 'why' or 'how' questions allows us to be more inclusive and open\-minded. Consider a scenario where the candidate may not be familiar with the best technical terminology yet can still convey the core knowledge and explain the solution's technical trade offs. This may be a great candidate who would very quickly close the terminology gap and be a valuable member of our team.
