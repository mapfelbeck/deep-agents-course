# Quality Engineering - Initial Interview

*NOTE: This content is adapted from the **QE Capability Initial Interview Training** deck, hosted on the **QE Director\+ Leadership** Teams site.* 

As Quality Engineering leaders, we spend a significant amount of time involved in recruiting activities. Successful hires for our capability come with different blends of skills and experience, but what they all have in common is a passion for modern software quality. 

During the initial interview, a QE leader must assess candidate interest, skills, experience, and potential team fit. They must make the final call whether to move forward with full interviews. 

## Preparing for the Candidate

Reserve time before the scheduled screen to prepare. The TA team should have provided a LinkedIn profile and/or resume for the candidate, as well as their notes from the recruiter screen. Take the time to review the candidate’s information, and look for points of interest you may incorporate into the screen. Things to look for include relevant experience, “job jumping”, writing skills, and specific technical skills, if listed. For LinkedIn profiles, look for shared connections that could be an input to the screen, especially if they are other Build employees. 

Consider where you might level the candidate, based on what you read. If needed, discuss proposed leveling with TA team to resolve any gray areas or to have context for the screen. 

## Handling Passive Candidates

The TA team will often indicate the candidate’s level of interest (passive vs. active), and you should flex your screen accordingly (sell vs. evaluate). For passive candidates, you may need to spend more time:

* **Sharing Information:** Provide passive candidates with the information they need to start to understand our company and our capability, which may mean spending 50% (or more in some cases) providing an overview and answering questions. The key is to meet candidates where they are – figuring that out can be a combination of asking the TA person who talked to them, and simply asking the candidate what they’d like you to cover.
* **Understanding Why They are Looking:** Even passive candidates have an agenda, perhaps something is missing where they work today. Find out why they don’t love their job, or what it is that would make them consider something new. Take note of this so that you can match it up with what we can offer and what we need, and incorporate it into your evaluation.
* **Moving at the Right Pace:** Not every passive candidate will be hooked by the dream of QE at Build (at least not on the first call!). That said, make it clear to candidates (especially ones you like) that they can take some time to consider and reconnect in future weeks, months, etc.  If appropriate, extend the offer to make a connection over time (“coffee every few months”).

## Leveling Candidates

The majority of our candidates are individual contributors. As you meet with the candidate, You should be familiar with [the career framework](https://twodegrees1.sharepoint.com/teams/Build/SitePages/Career-Framework.aspx), and have real examples of existing team members that you can choose from for comparison. Be sure to include your level recommendation in the notes you share back with TA. 

As a rule of thumb:



| **Engineer** | **Senior Engineer** | **Architect** | **Senior Architect** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Acts as a velocity contributor, working under the guidance of a senior QE team member. They are very rarely staffed as a single QE on a project. | Acts as a strong velocity contributor and emerging velocity multiplier. Has usually built at least one area of expertise. May be staffed solo on a delivery team. | Acts as a strong velocity multiplier, setting the direction for testing activities. Frequently asked to contribute velocity as well as overseeing the work of other QEs on a delivery team. | Acts as a velocity expert, building quality strategy and driving alignment with the client. May occasionally provide velocity as needed, but is typically focused on enabling others and driving the quality effort across the entire team. |

During the initial screen, it may be a bit early to talk about leveling with a candidate directly, especially before skills screening occurs. Simply inform the candidate that as they proceed through our process, we’ll have conversations about role/title/level at the appropriate time before any offer is extended.

## Structure of the Initial Interview

A typical candidate screen will align to the following:

* **Introductions \+ Settling In:** Take ownership of getting the conversation started, and creating a comfortable setting for the candidate. They are more nervous than you are. Help them to a point where they can be themselves, including chatting a bit and sharing an agenda for the conversation.
* **Share your Background:** Why did you join Slalom? What do you do at Slalom? What excites you about your day to day role, the people on our team, our clients, etc. ?  Not everyone looks at your LinkedIn before a screen, but even if they have, share something that goes beyond your work history.
* **QE within Slalom Build:** Spend some time describing how we work, how QE at Build is likely different than where most people work, and help them see what “day in the life” looks like.
* **Candidate Story \+ Questions:** Initial screens should allow a candidate with at least \~60% of the time allotted to share their story, and ask questions about the opportunity. Provide some direction to candidates on what you’d like to know about their experience. Take notes in some manner that will be shared with TA and the hiring team.
* **Closing the Interview:** In many cases, you may close the interview with limited time available to discuss next steps in detail, or to provide the candidate with a yes/no on moving forward. At a minimum, always thank the candidate for their time, and share some appreciation for their willingness to get to know you. Point them to the TA team member they are working with as a next step. When you have an exceptional interaction with a candidate and know you want to move forward, talk about it openly with them to build some momentum and show your interest.

As an interviewer, you should adjust the screen as needed to suit the candidate’s background and target level. If they are a referral, you may want to dig in a bit further about what they already know about Slalom. 

## Describing QE Within Slalom Build

Sharing a crisp, concise story about the way we work can differentiate our opportunity in the job marketplace, providing a candidate with a clear picture of the capability they may join. A good approach is to walk through a hypothetical project scenario from Discovery to Transition, to demonstrate what our work typically resembles. Include the full Build story as well as the QE components to highlight the interaction model on our projects. 

Some things to review regularly as an input to telling this story:

* QE Discipline Overview (headcount, clients, etc.)
* QE Core Principles (concepts we feel strongly about)
* Role of QE in Agile and at Build

Points to highlight as you share details with a candidate:

* We do project\-based, cross\-disciplinary client work across a variety of technology solutions
* QE is an integrated set of activities while building vs. retroactively applying test automation
* Strong community of QE professionals to lean on, and to contribute to
* Broad variety of tools, frameworks, methods, etc. to achieve our goals
* All of the above points lead to accelerated learning, a dynamic environment, and plenty of fun!
* All of the above require curious, motivated, enthusiastic people!

Note:  You don’t need to oversell, and you should be realistic about some of the challenges that we face on the ground while working with clients, budgets, and various other challenges.  

## Assessing Technical Skills

While we can’t do a deep evaluation of technical skills in the initial screen, it is important to do a reasonable amount of “tech screening” at this stage. Many candidates fail during the full tech screen interview, so it’s important to gain a credible understanding of where the candidate’s technical skills are today. 

Ways to assess this include:

* **Asking about a specific project :** Asking a candidate to go deep into a specific, recent project is a great way to get insight into a candidate’s technical skills.  Ensure you are steering them to discuss specific contributions they made, and that this is more than just a reiteration of their resume.
* **Asking about a relevant technology:** Does the candidate have experience with a specific technology or framework that we commonly use?  Ask the candidate to explain how it works, how it compares to alternates, what they like or dislike about it, what features they wish it had, or how they used it to solve a specific problem.
* **Get them to state an opinion:** There are many viewpoints and opinions in this field.  What do they feel passionate about?  What do they wish they could change?  What frustrates them in their current role?  Getting a candidate to open up about these things often leads to insightful conversation.



| **Example Questions** | **Warning Signs** |
| --- | --- |
| * “Talk me through the most technically challenging work you’ve done recently…” * “If you could build that framework again, what would you do differently?” * “I see you’ve used framework X, what do you like about it?” * “I see you’ve done a lot of UI automation, how does Selenium work?” * What do you think about story estimation?  How does it impact QE?” * If I put you in charge of your current company, what would you change?” | * Consistently speaking of team accomplishments, not individual contributions * Answering tangential questions instead of the question asked * Restating resume bullets without demonstrating deeper knowledge of a topic * Giving rehearsed or scripted answers without evidence of critical thinking * A lack of passion, spark, or innate interest when discussing quality concepts * Desire to use QE as a “stepping stone” into software engineering |

## Documenting Your Notes

A critical part of the hiring process is the work required during \+ after the screen to accurately capture notes and share them with the TA team for review, and store them in JobScience for future use with that candidate.  These notes also become a tool to help us evolve candidate questions, desired attributes, and interview format (i.e. learn by observation of others).

Core components of well\-documented notes:

* **Timely**.  Notes should be documented and shared with TA within 1\-2 days of screen (the sooner the better)
* **Templated**.  Notes should follow our common template for consistency.
* **Balanced**.  There needs to be enough detail to justify the rating and recommendation you provide, while being reasonable in length to protect your time.  Typically, drafting a good set of notes usually takes at least 25 minutes.


	+ **Pro Tip**:  Book 30 minutes after your interview to get the notes done immediately!
* **Consumable**.  These notes become an input to future rounds for candidates that move forward, and the next screener needs to easily understand what was found, how the candidate was leveled, and what items to look into, etc.

These notes and their circulation amongst our leadership team become the basis for consistency in how we evaluate and level candidates over time.
