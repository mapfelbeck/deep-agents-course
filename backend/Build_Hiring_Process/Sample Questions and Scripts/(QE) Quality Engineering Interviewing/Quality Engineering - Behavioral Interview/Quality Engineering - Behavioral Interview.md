# Quality Engineering - Behavioral Interview

* **QE Academy Interviewing Playbook**, on the **QE Principal\+** Teams site

In the Behavioral Interview(s), we are assessing candidates based on their experience. We approach this by asking them to provide specific examples of how they have demonstrated certain behaviors, knowledge, skills, and abilities. Rather than relying on first impressions, gut feelings, or reactions to hypothetical situations, it relies on descriptions of past behaviors in order to predict future job performance.

## Preparing for the Candidate

Review the feedback from the initial interview and the candidate’s resume. Both of these will be provided for you by the TA team and included in the calendar invite. Scan the written feedback for observations to explore further, since the initial screener is unlikely to have gone into them in depth. Look for areas of experience that are important to the Build model \- working in teams, cross\-discipline communication, etc. 

Be prepared to be flexible. QE technical skills interviewers frequently run out of time to cover some topics, requiring the behavioral interviewer to perform a “hybrid” interview. During the interviewer handoff, the technical interviewer may identify areas that need further follow\-up. These could be technical in nature (“*We didn’t cover creating a test strategy*”) or could be a behavioral characteristic that they’ve flagged (“*I sense that this person might be looking to move into software engineering long term.*”). Frequently, the interviewer can create a question that assesses both technical skills and behavioral characteristics.

## Considerations for Quality Engineering

Because Build is oriented toward cross\-capability teams, we look for many of the same characteristics that all Build capabilities look for.

There are situations and experiences that tend to be more prevalent with Quality Engineering candidates. These can be useful areas to dig into in order to understand how the candidate has navigated them in the past. These include:

* Companies that organize into silos of software engineering and software quality, including the “Test Center of Excellence” model
* Software engineering teams that claim to be “Agile”, but operate with very low proximity between dev and test activities (separate test sprints, testers not being ga part of planning / estimating, etc.)
* Common industry ratios of dev to test are usually about 3:1 or 4:1
* Software quality is not taught to a significant degree in computer science or software engineering programs; quality engineers frequently say they “stumbled into it by accident.”
* Build Quality Engineering is not as closely aligned to technology stacks as Build Software Engineering is (e.g. Modern Web, Java vs. .NET, etc.). QEs may have to navigate a less familiar technology stack on a project, which can create problems if not managed well.

Keep in mind that a behavioral interview should not solely focus on QE domain\-specific questions. Take a balanced approach. 

## Detailed Examples of Behavioral Questions

The below questions are *examples* of the types of QE\-oriented questions we might normally ask. As always, these should be used as inspiration for the questions that we actually ask candidates, and they should be tailored appropriately to the candidate’s resume and experience. 



| Pressure to cut corners on qualityCommunication, Conflict Management, Quality Strategy, Core Principles**Question**: Tell me how you’ve managed situations where you are being asked to cut corners or make a heroic effort in order for a user story to move to Done?**Why this question:** Even in the best teams will run into situations where a deadline conflicts with the need to validate and maintain quality. A common example is where user stories are not testable until late in a sprint. Another frequent scenario is cutting test automation requirements, or not adhering to best practices for devops integration. **Looking for**: Good communication and conflict resolution skills are a must in high pressure situations. A QE must learn to make the right tradeoffs when necessary, and work with the team to improve their practices. The more that a team works in alignment with the “QE Core Principles”, the better chance of success. **Red flags**: Relying on heroic efforts to always save the day. Inflexibility even when it’s appropriate. An “us vs. them” mindset. Architect / Senior Architect candidates need to show that they can strategically address these types of issues from the very beginning. Answers are generic, avoiding specifics even when asked. | **Expert Answer**:* Designs strategies to manage common situations from the very beginning of a project * Proactively communicates with client, with Build team, BPO, and other stakeholders * Manages complex / high\-visibility issues and negotiates resolution in partnership with SO/SA * Identifies team\-external behavioral patterns and works with appropriate stakeholders to resolve  **Advanced Answer**:* Identifies potential concerns before escalation and works to mitigate * Manages escalated situations and negotiates appropriate tradeoffs * Uses techniques like division of labor to minimize heroic efforts * Identifies behavioral patterns within the Build team and works to resolve successfully  **Base Answer**:* Self\-manages heroic effort as the exception, not the rule * Clearly communicates in high pressure situations * Can identify the problem and suggest solutions going forward |
| --- | --- |



| Improving QualityQuality Strategy, Core Principles**Question**: How have you worked to improve quality on your current team?*(Note that this is different than the previous question about cutting corners, but does have some overlap in terms of what we might be looking for)***Why this question**: This question can be interpreted many different ways, depending on candidate level and experience. It provides insight into how the candidate defines “quality.” Quality Engineering requires an Agile, team\-based approach steeped in good engineering practices. Quality Engineers provide their expertise to improve quality at a team\-wide level, rather than acting as “Quality Gatekeepers.”**Looking for**: A strong sense of how to define quality according to metrics, practices, and principles. An ability to impact quality before the software is written and is testable. Architects / Senior Architects should advocate for and drive improvements at a team level. **Red flags**: Loosely defined concepts of “quality.” Strongly advocates for outdated metrics like defect density or tester bug counts. Overly focused on “this is what we should do” vs. what they have done to date. A dogmatic focus on quality that leads to conflict. A lack of drive to improve, taking an “it is what it is” mentality. | **Expert Answer**:* Develops and drives a tailored quality strategy that evolves over time * Creates the key metrics, processes and patterns for defining quality * Identifies team\-external behavioral patterns and works with appropriate stakeholders to resolve  **Advanced Answer**:* Manages solution\-level ownership of quality * Identifies and reports on key metrics, processes, and patterns used to define quality * Identifies behavioral patterns within the Build team and works to resolve successfully * Improves quality through contributions during backlog grooming, sprint planning, etc.  **Base Answer**:* Has task\-level ownership of quality * Understands basic metrics and Agile processes * demonstrated self\-improvement in skills, approach, etc. * Participates in backlog grooming, sprint planning, etc. |
