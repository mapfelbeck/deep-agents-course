# Solution Ownership – Technical Interviewing (i.e. finding the purple unicorns)

 

The SO team believes that hiring great Solution Owners is a balance between Technical and Behavioral skills. Finding the right people means assessing multiple qualities. As a team we have put together the following document to lead our Technical interviewing. The questions in the first list are MUST ASK so that we get a consistent screening of candidates. In the subsequent sections are additional questions that the team has developed and that screeners can add as appropriate. At the bottom of the document is the working notes that lead the team to develop these specific questions.

 

Use this document to take your notes during the interview then copy/paste everything in Section 1 into the JobScience mail.

| On a scale of 1\-5 (1 being low, 5 being high), how would you rank yourself in terms of agile skills and experience? | Looking to establish the candidate’s own perception of their skills and experience in scrum. | What is your favorite scrum ceremony and why? | Open\-ended question intended to assess if the candidate knows their ceremonies, the intent behind them, and how to best run them. If they don’t have a favorite, the recommended ceremony to dig into is planning since it touches a number of best practices and elements we consider important (e.g. estimating in pts of complexity, understanding velocity and capacity, DoR, team commitment, etc.) If the candidate doesn’t have a favorite, can jump into the “least favorite scrum ceremony” question as a foil. | All |

| When you started a new project, what did you want to accomplish by the end of your first day, your first week, and your first month? | **SA –** Helps identify how a candidate thinks about approaching their role as requirements analyst on a team. Are they thinking both tactically (i.e. what tool and I going to use to capture requirements) and strategically (i.e. do I understand the product vision well enough to prioritize and focus my requirement gathering sessions)? Specifics that are good to hear:1. Proactively seeks to understand product vision in order to inform their requirements gathering approach 1. Plans and executes approach to gathering requirements from key stakeholders 1. Is able to organize requirements into epics, features, and stories 1. Knows when to start writing stories (i.e. doesn’t wait until all requirement sessions are complete)   Red flags include:1. Candidate isn’t a self\-starter or proactive 1. Does not give strong indications of being curious or seeking to understand 1. Doesn’t appear to be looking ahead and planning work to meet deadlines   **SO/SSO –** Helps identify how a candidate thinks about getting a project team up and running. Are they thinking both tactically (i.e. near term) and strategically (i.e. future planning)? Are they forming, storming, norming, and performing? By asking the question focused on “you” vs the team, it can be determined if the candidate thinks about the team first and how their responsibilities are all about setting the team and client up for success, or are they thinking about themselves first (e.g. I have to get this deliverable done because it’s required or I have to do this kick\-off meeting because the checklist says I have to). Specifics that are good to hear:1. Gets the whole team together, including the client, for a kick\-off in order to make introductions, review the overall vision, risks, and issues, and rally the team around the effort (this applies to both Discover and Deliver) 1. Takes steps to get information flowing (i.e. requirements, architecture, etc.) with a sense of urgency (not panic) 1. Starts to establish the governance foundation: team cadence of stand\-ups, weekly status, risks \& issues, open (and frequent) communications with the client PO or stakeholders 1. A general pattern of actions and expectations showing that the candidate understands the whole concept of forming, storming, norming, and performing and that teams have to go through this pattern in order to get established and evolve to high performance   Red flags include:1. Thinking of selves first (i.e. follows a checklist first and doesn’t think of the team) 1. Doesn’t work towards establishing foundations of collaboration and trust with client and within the team  Isn’t thinking about what needs to happen next week and next month when planning out and executing on what’s happening today | All |
| On a recent project, what were some of the challenges faced? What were the impacts and outcomes of those challenges? What role did you play in their resolution? Do they understand that risks and issues are different and require different approaches to mitigating vs resolving? Specifics that are good to hear:1. The candidate understands the difference between risks and issues and deals with them differently (i.e. different approaches, different communications, potentially different sense of urgency, etc.) 1. Candidate focuses on clear, honest communications about both and is able to set client/stakeholder expectations accordingly 1. Candidates approach, tenor, communications, etc. changes when a risk becomes reality and is an issue.   Red flags include:1. Doesn’t understand the basic differences between risks and issues and how to deal with them 1. When in a leadership position, the candidate didn’t take ownership of the mitigation strategy or issue resolution 1. The candidate shows a pattern of not recognizing risks and doesn’t effectively mitigate them 1. The candidate shows a pattern of not effectively managing client expectations around risks and issues or is a poor communicator re: risks and issues.   Note: While risks and issues are typically managed at the SO and SSO level, SA candidates should also be familiar with the differences between risks and issues at their level. They should also be experienced in managing those risks and issues at their level. | All |

| Tell me about a time when your client or primary stakeholder (e.g. PO) had an issue with your team’s velocity after your first sprint. How did you handle that? Does the candidate know that velocity is established over multiple sprints, not just the first (or one) and can they defend this position with the client in a way that is constructive? Red flags include:1. Candidate doesn’t effectively manage expectations of the client that velocity is established over time, not the first sprint 1. Candidate bends to client pressure 1. | Tell me about a time when your team’s velocity shifted significantly mid\-delivery. What was the shift? What was the root cause? How did you adjust? | Have you ever mixed agile approaches within the same project team (e.g. used both a Kanban board and flow for some aspects of the work and scrum for the rest)? | Helps assess the breadth and depth of both a candidate’s knowledge and experience. If they can give an example of where they successfully merged the approaches (i.e. identified the risks and mitigated them, were able to optimize the team, etc.), then they’re most likely pretty strong technically. If they weren’t able to or haven’t, it could be an indication that they’re not very experienced, are inflexible, or may be limited in their ability to come up with creative solutions to solving problems or optimizing their teams. Red flags include:1. Responses along the lines of “Never cross the streams”  Examples of merging approaches unsuccessfully or without realizing risks they imposed to the team | SOSSO |
