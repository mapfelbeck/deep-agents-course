# Technical Interview

# Overview

The Technical Interview \- formerly referred to as a white boarding session or technical in\-person interview \- takes the candidate into a deeper technical discussion than is possible in the Initial Interview. In this interview, the interviewer is working with the candidate to understand how they think through technical problems to understand their Technical Proficiency as well as their Persistence and Curiosity in the face of unknown technical challenges.

For engineering candidates, this conversation will include diagramming and coding exercises. This interview can be a challenge because there are a number of topics that are important to cover. It is crucial that the interviewer come to this interview prepared in order to ensure that you are exploring the correct topics based on the candidate’s experience and feedback from the Initial Interview.

This page provides detailed information on how Technical Interviews are coordinated and provides guidance for how these interviews are conducted across capability areas. Capability\-specific processes, scripts and interview guidance links are provided in each section.

# Preparation

A successful technical interview is dependent on the interviewer’s preparation. It’s important for the interviewer to understand:

* The hiring organization’s needs.
* The candidate’s primary technical focus and skills to tailor your questions.
* Their target skill level needed (e.g., How much of a hands\-on leadership, consulting skills, ability to take on discovery, etc…).
* What areas of focus or open questions were raised during the initial Interview?

# Interviewing for Aptitude

We are seeking team members with a baseline level of **Technical Proficiency** as well as the P**ersistence and Curiosity** to drive their own **Continuous Growth**. We use the following techniques to determine whether a person has the aptitude to grow in our team environments.

## Definition of Aptitude

* We define Aptitude as the readiness or quickness in learning and applied understanding.


	+ Competency is what we explicitly currently interview for, i.e. a person's current knowledge and command of specific subjects, and their ability to demonstrate this knowledge.
	+ Aptitude goes beyond current knowledge and focuses on a candidate's ability to a) acquire knowledge in the future that they do not currently have and b) apply existing knowledge to new situations.
	+ Strong aptitude in a candidate would make us feel more confident that they can easily make up shortcomings in competency once they get exposed to new knowledge and work with team members with more experience than themselves.
	
	
		- Ian Cook: “We hire for potential, not pedigree”

## General Guidance:

* If a candidate is unable to answer multiple questions in a specific category (OOP, Algos, Data Structures, ...), ask an aptitude\-based question in that category (for example: give the candidate the answer to one of the previous questions and ask follow\-up questions).
* If we have reservations about a candidate’s competency, then aptitude can be a differentiator.
* Interviewers should look for understanding to questions, such as real life examples, rather than simple definition\-type answers.


	+ E.g. not just what an Interface is, but why and when would I use it instead of an Abstract class

## Guide to asking questions to go beyond competency

* [Data Structure Example](https://slalom.atlassian.net/wiki/spaces/NDTS/pages/1811677269/Sample+Phone+Screen+Questions#Data-Structures): a candidate does not know about HashMaps \- once you explain them, are they able to come up with scenarios in which a HashMap would be a better data structure to use than an Array?
* [Algorithm Example](https://slalom.atlassian.net/wiki/spaces/NDTS/pages/1811677269/Sample+Phone+Screen+Questions#Algorithms): a candidate does not know the difference between a bubble sort and a quick sort \- once you explain the difference in the basic mechanisms for sorting, can they reason about why one is more efficient than the other?
* [OOP Example](https://slalom.atlassian.net/wiki/spaces/NDTS/pages/1811677269/Sample+Phone+Screen+Questions#OOP): a candidate cannot clearly define "encapsulation" \- once you explain it to them, can they articulate the benefits of it and situations in which they would want to take advantage of it.

## Logistics

This in\-depth technical conversation takes place either in person at a build location or virtually using approved Slalom Build Collaboration tools and technology. The TA team will manage the coordination for all interview cycles. The interviewing team will debrief to make incremental decisions as to the candidate’s progress throughout the interview.

## In\-Person coordination

When done in person, this will be the first conversation of in\-person interview rounds that involve both skills and behavioral interviews. This is a low\-tech conversation built around the candidates ability to express ideas and experience via diagrams or pseudocode via the white board. 

* The Interviewer will receive a set of meeting invites and messages from the TA team for each candidate. These materials will include


	+ Candidate details \- Candidate’s name, compensation and TA’s skills interview
	+ Calendar appointment for the interview (link)
	+ Interview Room/Space Coordination (link) \+ Debrief location
	+ You will receive an email “Evaluation Packet with Resume” at midnight the morning of the interview \- this will include a resume, all previous submitted feedback and content on “how we hire” Once you reach the "How we Hire\- Guidance for Interviewers" \& pages below, please disregard that specific content. It pertains to our local recruiting framework.
* The interviewer should review all candidate materials including their résumé/CV and prior conversation feedback. The interviewer must prepare appropriate questions to assess the candidate’s ability to apply to their skills and technology for the target role and level.
* Interviewer should bring a notepad or laptop with hard copies of the candidate materials provided by the TA team. They must ensure that the room has sufficient supplies to whiteboard (markers, whiteboard space, etc.).
* The interviewer will meet and interview the candidate then sync\-up with TA and next interviewer to debrief.

# Conversation

The objective coming out of the Technical Interview is to determine whether the candidate has the technical abilities to perform in the job. While the content of the interview will vary from capability to capability, core constructs around role levelling and soft skills don’t change. 

Depending on the role level, areas that we are looking out for in the Technical Interview include:

* **Technical Proficiency:** This is the **table\-stakes** criteria for the Technical Interview: Does the candidate possess the depth \& breadth of technical skills (depending on level) to perform effectively and efficiently in the role, today? This can be determined through the quality, speed, and confidence in their answers.
* **Potential:** In a team\-oriented environment, with appropriate practice and peer support, what is the longer term upside of a candidate? Do they demonstrate persistence \& curiosity as they work through Technical Interview problems? This can be determined through the creativity of their answers, and their curiosity and challenging mindset demonstrated throughout the interview conversation.
* **Consulting Presence:** This is particularly relevant in client\-facing levels (i.e., Architect\+) where candidates are expected to drive meetings and interactions with clients. How polished are their white\-boarding skills, how strong are their communication and empathy?
* **Team Collaboration:** Throughout the in person interview, we’re evaluating what it would be like to have this candidate on our teams. Is this candidate someone who can be successful on a team project?

# Feedback

We use the same assessment summary email template to capture feedback for all Interviews.

It is important to remember that the Technical Interview intends to assess the candidate’s technical proficiency: their ability to perform the technical activities of the role they are interviewing for. Consider staff\-ability and potential for development velocity on day one as a litmus test that all candidates should pass for their role. As an example, it may seem hard to pass up on an intelligent person who is excellent with Mathematics. It is best that we do if they have not been building software that delivers customer outcomes and would need significant ramp up to deliver development velocity.

Every one of us will be learning every day on the job, but for the most part, the expectation is that those investments can be made while providing proper velocity on projects.

Capture feedback during the interview using the templates found on the assessment summary page. Let the interviewee know you will be taking notes during the interview. Once the interview is over, always thank the candidate.

After the interview, you will have a debrief with the recruiter, hiring manager, behavioral interviewer, and others in the Build organization that may be required. Use your notes to summarize your understanding of the candidate: strengths, weaknesses, and development areas. The team will be looking for a ‘Yes’ or ‘Do Not Proceed’ from you, and your notes using the assessment summary email template will be your data to back your recommendation.


> **Pro Tip**: If you need help making a decision, visit the Go/No Go Guidance page in the Assessment Guidance section.

You will have received an automated email requesting your feedback from the recruiter or Job Science. Use the link in that email to provide your feedback in Job Science before the end of the day of your Technical Interview session. This is critical to keeping the recruiting experience moving forward for the candidate. Please fill out the survey and copy/paste your notes in the comments box.
