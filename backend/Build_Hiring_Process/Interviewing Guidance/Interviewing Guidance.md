# Interviewing Guidance

In order to gauge if a candidate can meet the expectations of their role, we need to rate their ability across the core areas of technical competency for our team. Our experience indicates that focusing on a technical screen as the first step will lead to a better candidate pool and higher success rate through the hiring process.

To effectively provide a rating for a candidate for a given area, we need to be able to:

* Cover enough questions on an area (breadth). This ensures that a single topic in an area doesn’t artificially represent that category
* Measure depth in an area. This ensures that the candidate has understanding and competency in that area which can go beyond easy interview preparation.

Given the amount of time you have to interview a candidate in each core area, a balanced mix of question types will enable you gauge both breadth and depth. This page serves as a guide in how to structure those questions and provides examples.

# Goals

* Accurately assess a candidates rating for a given category. Ratings are shown in the table below.
* Gauge understanding for a candidate in an area.


	+ Limit trivia questions
	+ Find out what the candidate understands
* Provide a framework for scaling an interview to meet a candidates depth. Some candidates can really shine and achieve Excellent ratings. We need to provide a question framework to scale for these situations.
* Our framework should also provide consistency in our interviews.
* Remove or reduce bias in interviews.

[Rating Scale](https://slalom.atlassian.net/wiki/spaces/NDTS/pages/68059453/Software+Engineering) 



| Rating | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Not Covered | You didn't talk about the subject, or the subject was not applicable. (If giving this rating, please include a brief phrase why, i.e. "Not covered (ran out of time)" |
| Poor | Consistently and/or significantly missed expectations for the role for which they are interviewing. This rating is also applicable if the candidate has no knowledge in an area. |
| Fair | Sometimes met and sometimes missed expectations for the role for which they are interviewing |
| Good | Consistently met and sometimes exceeded expectations for the role for which they are interviewing |
| Excellent | Consistently and/or significantly exceeded expectations for the role for which they are interviewing |

# Question Guidelines

To gauge breadth and depth, we recommend mixing the types of questions in each category. This will allow for touching on the main areas of a category as well as going deep in a couple areas. The following chart provides examples for question type and the expected time commitment.

In order to cover the breadth of topics that we are seeking to cover, an initial screen should have a mix of question types for each category with a Low and Medium Interview time commitment. If time permits, a conceptual question can fit into a category to further understand a candidates strength. It is not recommended to ask problem solving questions during an initial screen unless you have ample time and have assessed the desired areas.



| **Question Type** | **Framing** | **Goal** | **Example** | **Interview time commitment** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Trivia | What | Quickly gauge base level of understanding | What is an interface? | Low |
| Practical | Why, How, When, Example | Look for practical understanding | Why would you use an interface?How do you use an interface?When is it appropriate to use an interface?Give me an example of when you used an interface? | Medium |
| Comparison | Compare or Contrast | Look for understanding options or what is a right solution | Compare and contrast an interface and an abstract class. | Medium |
| Conceptual | Application of ideas or concepts | Look for deep understanding on a topic | Explain the role of interfaces in design patterns. | High |
| Problem solving | Solve the following… | Assess problem solving skills | How might you design a media player object model using interfaces? | High |

# Initial Screen Guidelines

* Prepare


	+ Prepare questions in advance
	+ Do not ask a question you do not know the answer to
	+ Structure your interview with a blend of question types
	+ Ensure camera and audio is working in advance
* Provide a positive candidate experience


	+ Be on time
	+ Be positive
	+ Introduce yourself and set expectations up front
	+ Be completely focused in the interview (remove distractions like slack or outlook)
	+ Answer their questions (sell Slalom)
* Interviewing


	+ Time is precious \- Do not rehash a candidate’s resume or go over their background in depth; 
	
	
		- Know their prior experience so you can meet them where they are
	+ If a candidate is stuck, move on
	+ Manage your time. Do not spend too much time in a category.
	+ Validate answers that seem off by asking a related follow up question.
* Assessing


	+ Take copious notes
	+ Reserve time for feedback
	+ Assess understanding not a checklist
	+ Seek advice from the interview alias or others. Collaborating is how we ensure we are doing this right consistently.
	+ Watch out for the affinity bias! Don’t allow your bias of liking a candidate skew your technical ratings.
